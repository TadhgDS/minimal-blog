Clean Code: A Handbook of Agile Software Craftsmanship

- Robert C. Martin

-- This is a great book and I'd recommend it to all junior developers that want to learn the basics of well designed software. It's also a good introduction to Test-Driven Development. Martin builds his examples incrementally throughout the book and it gives a great indication of what good software design is supposed to look like in practice. He even builds the basics of the JUnit library, and it's nice to see how all sophisticated, well developed projects start off with a few rudimentary ideas.



The Easy Way to Stop Smoking: Join the Millions Who Have Become Nonsmokers Using the Easyway Method

- Allen Carr

-- This is actually the second time I've given up smoking, both times with the help of this book. The first time I got a bit too confident and felt there was no way I could fall back into the trap, which inevitably did happen. This time, I'm in a better position. The way I see it, the book simply puts forward a bunch of arguments for why smoker's smoke, why they started and what keeps them hooked. It also convinces you that quitting is actually very easy. The arguements are more or less rephrased and put it different contexts for the length of the book. This doesn't sound very effective but it is. The idea is that by the end of the book, you can't help but feel that smoking is silly, and the ideas are so intrenched that when you start to feel pangs and question yourself, you are armed with these arguments and can quickly shut out the little voices as being nonsensicle.



John Adams

- David McCullough

-- This is an excellent biography by an excellent biographer. This book shines light on the fortitude and unwavering tenacity of the man who would become the 2nd president of the united states. My understanding is that Adams has often been overshadowed by his predecessor (George Washinton) and his successor (Thomas Jefferson) due to their more iconic/glitzy roles in history, those being the general of the union army and writer of the declaration of independence, respectively. This book is a testament to the man who, moreso than any other, fought for the cause of independence both at home and abroad, rallying support and gaining recognition for the fledgling state.
